Research-informed reference

What the evidence supports

  • Strength: train all major muscle groups on 2+ days each week. More days can make the same weekly work easier to spread out, but frequency alone is not magic.

  • Mobility: there is no universal ideal. Two or three short sessions is a practical habit goal.

Strength, cardio, and mobility: the basics for a well-rounded week.
01 · Strength training

Stay strong as you age

Strength work helps maintain bone, muscle, and everyday function—stairs, groceries, getting up from the floor, and catching yourself after a stumble.

2+ daysmuscle-strengthening work each week
Slow gainbone adapts over months and years
Keep movingstrength, balance, and function reinforce each other

About the “2% per year” idea: bone adaptation is real but not uniform or guaranteed. In a meta-analysis of older-adult resistance-training studies, average changes were roughly 0.6% at the total hip and lumbar spine across 12–52 weeks. More targeted, higher-intensity or impact-inclusive programs can show different results. The practical win is often slowing age-related loss while getting substantially stronger.

Train consistently. It supports strength, balance, and mobility, but it cannot guarantee injury prevention.

02 · Cardio

Build your engine

Walk, jog, bike, swim, row, hike, dance, or play a sport. It all counts when it gets your breathing and heart rate up.

150–300 minmoderate cardio each week
or 75–150 minvigorous cardio each week
Start smalleven 10–15 minutes is a valid beginning

Regular aerobic activity supports longevity and is linked with lower risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, and several other chronic conditions. An American Heart Association scientific statement reports average blood-pressure reductions of about 3–4 mmHg with increased activity—meaningful at a population level, while individual changes vary.

Blood clots: long periods of limited movement are a clot risk factor. Getting up, walking, and using your calf muscles during long sitting or travel supports circulation, but cardio is not a substitute for medical prevention or treatment. Seek urgent care for possible clot symptoms, and ask a clinician about personal risk factors.

Start with three easy 20–30 minute sessions. Build slowly. At a moderate pace, you can speak in short sentences.
Sources: American Heart Association activity recommendations · AHA blood-pressure statement · CDC clot-risk information
03 · Mobility & stretching

Keep moving well

Flexibility is range of motion. Mobility is control through that range. Both make training and daily movement easier.

2–3 daysof flexibility work per week
30 seca practical static-hold target for most adults
~60 sectotal per target muscle group

Thirty seconds is a useful standard, not a magic threshold. General guidance is 10–30 second static holds for most adults and 30–60 seconds for many older adults, repeated until you accumulate roughly 60 seconds per muscle group. Use a mild-to-moderate stretch sensation, breathe, and avoid bouncing or pushing through pain.

Use dynamic movements before lifting or cardio to prepare for the activity. Reserve longer static holds for after training or a separate mobility session. Stretching can improve range of motion; it is not a guarantee against injury, so pair it with strength and sensible load progression.

Start with 2–4 tight areas after training. Do two calm 30-second holds per side. Do not force the stretch.
Sources: static-stretching dose meta-analysis · ACSM flexibility guidance
